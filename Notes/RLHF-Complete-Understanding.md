# RLHF: Complete Understanding

**Written:** March 11, 2026 
**Based on:** "Learning to Summarize from Human Feedback" (Stiennon et al., 2022)
**Purpose:** Understanding RLHF methodology and demonstrating how this work implements RLHF principles in practice

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) solves a fundamental problem in AI training. Currently, models are trained to predict human text using supervised learning, but we want them to generate genuinely high-quality outputs.  These goals do not align. 
Supervised learning models contained numerous problems: hallucinations, factual errors, and distributional shift. Distributional shift occurs because models are trained on human-written text but must generate their own text in deployment. This misalignment means models fail in ways they weren’t prepared for.  
RLHF solves these problems by training on the model’s own generations, rather than on human text so the model can learn in the right context. The goal in training models using RLHF is alignment, not just accuracy. This means if we want a helpful, honest, and fair summarization from AI, the model must be trained with human feedback on what we actually prefer. The result is models that align with human values- they do what we want them to do.  
RLHF uses reward modeling as part of its training process. Reward modeling starts by collecting human preferences by having humans directly compare two model outputs on the same input and choose which is better. This builds a dataset of preferences. Models then learn from the comparisons why one output was preferred over the other. This process teaches the reward model to recognize human values and preferences. 
Once the reward model understands what we value, it scores outputs automatically. The big policy model is then trained to maximize these reward scores using a PPO algorithm. This cycle- collecting human feedback, retraining the reward model, optimizing the policy- can be repeated. With each new cycle, the policy improves by generating high-quality outputs that align with human preferences.  
When using reward modeling, quality is most important. Good feedback from humans translates to a good reward model, which then translates to a good policy. However, poor quality feedback from humans will result in a poor reward model, and ultimately poor policy. RLHF directly correlates with high or low quality. 
To avoid poor quality feedback, researchers and labelers developed a standard to adhere to produce high quality results. This required labelers to receive detailed instructions with examples of good and bad summaries, clearly explained criteria, and hands-on support. This includes labeler chat support for questions, clarity on edge cases, regular feedback on their work, and continuous monitoring of agreement rates. This resulted in a 77% consistency in agreement between researcher and labeler. Researchers only agreed with each other 73% of the time, making the 77% labeler-researcher agreement remarkable-labelers matched expert researchers’ judgments better than experts matched each other.  
There are some situations where the rules are not clear. This is known as an edge case. An edge case is a situation where the evaluation criteria are not clear, or multiple interpretations could be valid. An example is when a post is confusing and summary paraphrases it differently. It becomes unclear if paraphrase is accurate. This becomes hard to judge, and different labelers may disagree or be unsure. This is when mismatches with the researchers can occur. These mismatches trigger an iteration- a repeat of the reward modeling cycle to improve the quality of the edge case by refining rubrics, clarifying the criteria for edge cases, and improving signal for the next round of training. 
In my current role working with AI, I use reinforcement learning and reward modeling to improve client AI systems. I am given data rows- or DRs- containing user input followed by one or more model response outputs. I then compare the client’s model against baseline models or competing models, and make my preference selection, known as reward modeling. Based on this preference selection, I then review the rubrics-the criteria that define what is “good”.  Once I’ve refined the rubrics based on my preferences, this data is returned to the client. They use this feedback to retrain their model, implementing improvements identified through my evaluation work. 
I have had the privilege of participating in multiple roles within my company, beginning as a labeler. As a labeler, I aim to improve client AI systems using reinforcement learning and reward modeling. Once reward modeling datasets are sent back to the client and their models are retrained, occasionally new issues arise. This triggers a new iteration to occur. 
I had the opportunity to work on a project that went through multiple iterations. The first focused on improving the quality and fairness of the rubrics themselves. The second iteration focused on identifying and fixing separate failure axes. I had to find failures and create new rubrics to prevent them in future model responses. This is the RLHF cycle in motion. 
My role expanded to include reviewer. As a reviewer, I had two responsibilities. First, I reviewed data rows for accuracy before they reached labelers, ensuring the data row had a confirmed failure to test. Secondly, I reviewed the quality of labelers’ work, providing feedback, both positive and constructive, to help them improve their evaluation quality. This quality control ensures the training signal remains strong. 
My work with AI models is critical to the RLHF cycle as my rubrics act as reward functions, my scoring as preference data, and my rubric refinement driving client policy optimization. Once this process is completed, my data is returned to the client for model training to begin again.  The evidence that my previous feedback worked appears when a new iteration begins - the previous iteration’s retraining was completed successfully and new issues now exist to address. This process - my high-quality feedback, the client’s retraining, and the emergence of new issues to solve- demonstrates RLHF created model alignment, the ultimate goal of this training method. 
As with anything, RLHF has drawbacks. First, this training process is expensive. Therefore, who can use this method and at what scale is not standard among all AI companies. This can cause inconsistencies in models depending on the financial stability of each company. Secondly, while RLHF can create high quality outputs based on human preference and fairness, the opposite can also be true. The models can be trained to manipulate people, generate toxic content, spread misinformation, and create unhealthy dependence on technology. This can cause both ethical and social problems and is very difficult to prevent. 
Reinforcement learning with human feedback is a powerful training tool that is used to solve a fundamental problem in AI- alignment with human preferences and expectations. Using expertly crafted, high-quality rubrics, models can learn what makes a response “good”. A good response is one that aligns with the criteria I define through my rubrics and evaluation. This type of training is not simply a theory, but a practical process used every day. I implement this process in every role I take on, which gives me complete understanding of how all the pieces work together. Understanding both the theory and practice of RLHF will serve me as I continue my journey in AI safety.

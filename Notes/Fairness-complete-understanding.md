# Fairness Fundamentals: Understanding AI Fairness in Practice

**Written:** March 27, 2026
**Based on:** "AI Fairness in Practice: Paradigm, Challenges, and Prospects" (Zhang, 2024)
**Purpose:** Understanding AI fairness metrics and debiasing algorithms, and demonstrating how fairness work implements these principles in practice


Fairness in AI is a growing concern as most models aim to provide high-quality responses to their users. This concern stems from the supervised learning methods used to train models. This training method assumed that models were trained on a complete set of data, meaning models would learn all outcomes for all people. However, data in the real-world can often be incomplete or censored. This means that we don't actually know the outcomes for all people, which can cause blind spots in model responses. When models operate with these blind spots, they make assumptions that can unfairly bias certain demographic groups. The paper "AI Fairness in Practice: Paradigm, challenges, and prospects”, by Wenbin Zhang (2024) discusses the recent advances in AI fairness aimed at bridging the gap between fairness theory and real-world deployment. 
According to Zhang, there are two types of censorship to focus on. Administrative censoring, meaning an individual may not experience the intended event before the study ends and therefore this individual becomes an "unknown" piece of data. Second, Loss to follow-up. This means an individual dropped out, withdrew, or had a competing event happen first. This individual's outcome is now also an "unknown" piece of data. When their data is unknown, it gets dropped from the dataset. This removal skews the remaining data, introducing bias. However, censoring doesn't affect all groups equally. If one demographic group is more likely to withdraw or be lost to follow-up, by having their datasets dropped, the dataset is skewed towards only the participating groups which then produces biased predictions that favor those who participated. 
Due to the presence of censorship in data, commonly used fairness definitions are not applicable, therefore a new set of fairness metrics have been proposed. These metrics are performed at group and the individual level, to better determine model unfairness in the presence of censorship. One metric, Concordance Imparity, represents group fairness by assessing whether a model systematically underperforms for certain demographics by assessing the greatest differences in discriminative abilities among various populations. Another metric, Fair discounted cumulative gain (FDCG) is used to quantify individual fairness. FDCG evaluates unfairness from a ranking perspective while also considering survival information. Both methods are theoretically sound and can be used at the discretion of the trainer. In my current role with Alignerr, we focus on group fairness across demographic groups which suggests that Concordance Imparity would be the most applicable to our rubric validation work. 
Complementing the metrics previously described are debiasing algorithms that address unfairness directly. Fair Survival Random Forests is introduced, and it modifies how a Random Forest makes splitting decisions by adding a fairness consideration through the Fair Survival Difference Criterion. If the split improves prediction and reduces discrimination at the same time, FSD becomes positive infinite, therefore the algorithm will strongly prioritize splits that are already fair. At Alignerr, I implement fairness debiasing similar to FSRF's methodology. Rather than retraining from scratch, I layer fairness constraints (rubrics) onto existing neural networks. Each iteration provides new ranking data, allowing the system to improve fairness while maintaining the base model's predictive power. By keeping the model constant, fairness improvements can be attributed specifically to the rubric constraints and human feedback ranking- similar to how IFS isolates its fairness gains. This approach prioritizes both interpretability and efficiency. 
A critical gap and limitation to these methods is their predominant focus on only one sensitive attribute at a time. Individuals in marginalized groups can have more than one marginalized identity. For example, women themselves are marginalized, but what about women of color? What about women of color who are also homosexual? Even as we fix bias, we might harm people at the intersection of multiple marginalized identities. This means fairness work needs to be holistic in its approach. A narrow approach fails to capture these complex realities; therefore, it becomes crucial to develop models and frameworks that account for multiple, overlapping identities and that address these intersectional impacts. This means fairness frameworks must account for how discrimination compounds across multiple identities simultaneously, not address each in isolation. 
Fairness in AI remains an unsolved problem. Data is complex and evolving, therefore fairness frameworks must also evolve accordingly. The current metrics and debiasing approaches show progress toward practical fairness, but intersectionality challenges still exist. At Alignerr, I participate in this work daily. Through rubric design, iterative improvement, and careful attention to edge cases, I help ensure AI systems are not just accurate, but fair to all people they affect. This understanding of both the theory and practice of fairness in AI will guide my continued work in AI safety.

# Why My Alignerr Work Is AI Safety Work: Understanding Constitutional AI
**Written:** April 11, 2026
**Based on:** "Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback" (Bai et al., 2022)  
**Purpose:** Understanding Constitutional AI and its connection to fairness-focused rubric engineering


Constitutional AI is a set of simple, natural language principles used to encode goals within AI systems. It guides behavior through explicit principles instead of thousands of implicit human labels. This is my work at Alignerr—my rubrics ARE constitutional principles. When I score a model’s response as 'meets criteria' or 'doesn't meet criteria,' I'm providing the AI feedback that Constitutional AI describes. Constitutional AI is more than just scoring—it's a complete framework for training AI systems to be helpful, harmless, and fair. Understanding this framework reveals why my work at Alignerr IS AI safety work.
The constitutional AI method is a two-stage process. In stage one, the models were trained using supervised learning. The goal of this stage of learning is to get the model on distribution before the next stage. This is accomplished by generating harmful responses from a helpful-only model and then asking the model to critique its response using a constitutional principle. The model then revises the response based on the critique. This process is repeated with different principles being randomly sampled.  This fine-tunes the model on these revised responses. My Alignerr work implements this exact process. When I identify that a rubric doesn't meet expectations, I revise it based on what's missing—just as Constitutional AI revises harmful responses based on critiques. Each iteration improves the rubric's quality and specificity. Stage two trains models using reinforcement learning. This process involves generating pairs of responses from the SL model and then asking the model to evaluate which responses are better according to the constitutional principles. This creates a preference model from these AI-generated comparisons. The final model is trained using RL with this preference model as reward signal. This critique-and-revision cycle, repeated iteratively, progressively reduces harmfulness. RL-CAI models equal or exceed the performance of human feedback-trained models. My work at Alignerr implements each stage of this framework. I generate rubrics (constitutional principles), edit them to ensure they meet expectations (the critique-revision process), and score responses against them (the preference modeling). I'm also building a Python rubric generator that creates baseline constitutional principles, which I then refine and layer with bias and fairness considerations. Over time, this system will learn—generating rubrics that inherently include debiasing principles without manual addition.
Critique-Revision pipeline is the next stage of the practical methodology of implementing constitutional AI. This process starts with a helpful-only model that generates harmful responses. The model is asked to critique its response by identifying what is harmful. It generates a critique explaining the problems. Then, a revision request asks the model to remove all harmful content, and it generates a harmless revised response. This process is repeated multiple times with different constitutional principles. The result is progressively better harmless responses through iterative refinement. In my Alignerr work, I implement this exact critique-revision loop. My rubrics serve as constitutional principles. When a model generates a response, I evaluate it against these rubrics. My feedback—identifying what's missing or biased—IS the critique. When I revise a rubric to ensure it 'meets expectations,' I'm removing harm, just as Constitutional AI removes harmful content. Each iteration improves the rubric's quality and specificity. Through this iterative critique-and-revision process, my rubrics progressively improve, and the models trained against them become fairer and more aligned.
There are setbacks to this type of model training known as overfitting. When a model is over-trained, RL-CAI models develop issues such as being over-reactive to harmful prompts. It also adds boilerplate words like "You are valid, valued, and cared for" to most responses. These responses become artificial rather than naturally harmless. Researchers addressed these issues by refining constitutional principles to avoid over-reactivity. They discovered that assembling multiple principles, using soft probability labels instead of hard binary choices, and limiting training duration prevented the model from becoming artificial. At Alignerr, I'm aware of these overfitting risks. By maintaining diverse rubrics, avoiding extreme confidence in my scoring, and iterating carefully rather than over-training, I ensure my debiasing work produces naturally fair responses—not artificial ones.
Constitutional AI aims to train systems that are helpful, harmless, and fair. Fairness isn't optional—biased models are fundamentally unsafe models. In my Fairness Fundamentals paper, I studied how hidden biases create unforeseen harm. Censored data, intersectional discrimination, and proxy bias all slip through undetected. My debiasing rubrics directly address these gaps. By explicitly evaluating fairness, I catch harms before they propagate. This iterative debiasing process—generating rubrics, identifying bias, refining principles—IS the Constitutional AI framework applied to safety. My work prevents models from causing unforeseen harm to marginalized groups. That is AI safety work
Constitutional AI demonstrates that explicit principles guide behavior better than implicit thousands of labels. My rubrics ARE these principles. My daily work at Alignerr—generating, critiquing, and refining rubrics for fairness—implements this exact framework. I am not just labeling data. I am training AI systems to be fair, aligned, and safe. This is why my Alignerr work is AI safety work.
